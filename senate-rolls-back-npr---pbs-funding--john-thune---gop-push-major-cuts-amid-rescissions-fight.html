Senate Rolls Back NPR & PBS Funding: John Thune & GOP Push Major Cuts Amid Rescissions Fight

The GOP's Rescission Wave Hits NPR and PBS: John Thune Leads Funding Rollbacks in 2025

In a striking turn of legislative strategy, the U.S. Senate, led by Republican Senator John Thune of South Dakota, has moved forward with a significant $9 billion rescission package, pulling funding from key areas including public broadcasting (NPR and PBS) and foreign aid programs. This bill has become a flashpoint for both fiscal conservatives and media advocates.

This legislative development, which passed narrowly in the Senate on July 17, 2025, has sparked intense debate over the future of public media, the role of independent journalism, and how America positions itself in terms of foreign diplomacy. The key focus has been a controversial $1.1 billion cut to the Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB), directly impacting NPR and PBS affiliates nationwide.

Background: Understanding the Rescission

Rescission is a rarely used legislative tool that cancels previously approved federal funds. The GOP majority, energized by recent electoral momentum, has weaponized this tool to reclaim unspent COVID-era funding and push back on Democratic budget priorities. At the center of this push is Senator John Thune, who has framed the move as restoring "fiscal discipline" and curbing what he calls "reckless appropriations."

  • $1.1 billion cut from CPB (affecting NPR and PBS)
  • $8 billion in foreign aid previously allocated for Ukraine, Gaza, and other global crisis zones
  • Additional cuts from environmental and social programs that were funded but not yet disbursed

The Senate Vote and Key Figures

The bill passed with a narrow 51–48 vote in the Senate. Notably, two Republican Senators—Susan Collins (ME) and Lisa Murkowski (AK)—broke with their party to vote against it, citing concerns about local journalism and rural access to emergency broadcasts.

The procedural steps to move the bill forward required a tie-breaking vote from Vice President JD Vance, highlighting the razor-thin political balance currently in the Senate.

Senator Mike Rounds (R-SD) voiced concern about the bill's impact on tribal and rural access to local news, even as he ultimately voted in favor. In contrast, Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer lambasted the measure as a "dangerous precedent" for bipartisan budgeting.

Impact on Public Broadcasting: What NPR and PBS Stand to Lose

Public media executives warn that the CPB cut could be catastrophic for local stations, especially those in rural and underserved communities.

NPR, which relies partly on CPB grants distributed to member stations, could see a major drop in coverage capability and regional reporting strength.

PBS, similarly, uses CPB support to fund educational programming and emergency alert systems, which are critical in areas with limited access to commercial networks.

According to insiders, if this bill becomes law:

  • Over 1,000 public media employees could be laid off
  • Dozens of rural stations could shut down
  • Educational programming for children (like "PBS Kids") might be scaled back

A joint statement from NPR and PBS executives emphasized that public broadcasting reaches 99% of American households, and this funding cut is “not just about media; it’s about democracy and access.”

Foreign Aid Cuts: Geopolitical Concerns

In addition to domestic implications, the bill targets nearly $8 billion in foreign aid, sparking concern from diplomats and international partners.

The cuts include funding for:

  • Ukraine’s defense and reconstruction
  • Gaza humanitarian aid
  • Global health initiatives, including anti-malaria and anti-HIV programs

While a $400 million allocation to PEPFAR (President’s Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief) was preserved following bipartisan negotiation, critics argue that the larger rollback undermines U.S. soft power and global leadership.

Political Dynamics: A Dangerous Fiscal Precedent?

The use of rescissions to override existing budgets has triggered alarm across the aisle.

Chuck Schumer warned that if this becomes routine, it could lead to a breakdown in the annual appropriations process, where bipartisan cooperation is essential.

“This is not about fiscal conservatism,” Schumer said during floor debate. “It’s about power, and silencing voices in media and diplomacy that some don’t want to hear.”

Progressives argue that targeting NPR and PBS is part of a broader culture war aimed at discrediting independent journalism. Conservatives, meanwhile, claim public broadcasting is biased and that tax dollars shouldn’t support it.

The House Outlook

The bill now moves to the House of Representatives, where Speaker Mike Johnson (R-LA) has indicated support. However, moderates in the GOP—especially those from rural districts—could resist if public backlash grows.
